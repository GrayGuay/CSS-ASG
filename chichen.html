About Chichén Itzá

Chichén Itzá is a large pre-Columbian archaeological site built by the Maya civilization located in the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico. It features iconic structures such as El Castillo, the Temple of the Warriors, and the Great Ball Court. The site demonstrates advanced architectural, astronomical, and cultural knowledge of the Maya people.

History

Chichén Itzá was a major economic, political, and religious center during the Terminal Classic period (c. AD 800–1000). The pyramid of El Castillo was used for astronomical and ceremonial purposes. You can explore more about Maya peoples and their history.

Timeline

600 AD – Early Settlement

Early Maya communities began settling in the region that would become Chichén Itzá.

800 -900 AD – Major Construction

Iconic structures like El Castillo and the Great Ball Court were built during this period.

1000 AD – Peak of Power

Chichén Itzá reached its peak of power and influence during this period becoming a major political, economic, and religious center.

1200 AD – Decline

The city gradually declined and was eventually abandoned by reasons still debated by historians.

19th–20th Century

Archaeological efforts helped restore and preserve the site.

Culture

Chichén Itzá played a vital role in Maya religious life. Many structures were aligned with astronomical events, reflecting the Maya belief in the connection between the heavens and human life.

El Castillo is dedicated to Kukulcán, the feathered serpent god, symbolizing renewal, agriculture, and divine power. Rituals, ceremonies, and games held at the site were deeply connected to Maya spiritual beliefs.

Fun Facts

Feature Details
Shadow During the spring and autumn equinox, the shadow on El Castillo appearsto form a moving serpent.
Ball Court The Great Ball Court is the largest and best-preserved ball court in ancient Mesoamerica.
Name The name “Chichén Itzá” means “At the mouth of the well of the Itza people.”
