Overview

The Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is an iconic symbol of Ancient Rome and one of the most famous landmarks in the world. Built between 70-80 AD, it could hold up to 50,000 spectators who gathered to watch gladiatorial contests, public spectacles, and theatrical performances. Located in the center of Rome, Italy, the Colosseum showcases the ingenuity of Roman engineering, with its complex system of arches, corridors, and seating arrangements. Learn more on Wikipedia.

History

The construction of the Colosseum began under Emperor Vespasian in 70 AD and was completed in 80 AD under his son, Emperor Titus. It was primarily used for gladiatorial games and public entertainment, reflecting the social and political culture of the Roman Empire. Over the centuries, the Colosseum suffered damage from earthquakes, stone-robbers, and natural wear, but restoration efforts have preserved much of its structure. Today, it stands as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a major tourist attraction, offering a glimpse into Ancient Roman life. You can explore more about Ancient Rome and its history.
