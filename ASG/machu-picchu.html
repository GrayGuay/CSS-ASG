Machu Picchu

Exploring the Place

Location of the Place

Overview

Machu Picchu is an ancient Incan citadel located high in the Andes Mountains of Peru, overlooking the Urubamba River valley. Built in the 15th century, it is renowned for its sophisticated dry-stone construction, terraced fields, and panoramic mountain views. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas." Learn more on Wikipedia.

Cultural and Religious Significance

Machu Picchu held deep cultural and spiritual importance for the Inca civilization. It is believed to have been a royal estate for Emperor Pachacuti and a sacred ceremonial center.

The site is carefully aligned with astronomical events such as solstices, reflecting the Inca belief in harmony between humans, nature, and the cosmos. Mountains surrounding Machu Picchu were considered sacred spirits known as Apus.

History

Machu Picchu was built around 1450 during the height of the Inca Empire, likely as a royal estate or religious site. It was abandoned a century later during the Spanish conquest, though the citadel was never discovered by Spanish conquistadors. The site remained largely unknown to the outside world until American historian Hiram Bingham brought it international attention in 1911. Today, Machu Picchu is one of the most visited archaeological sites in the world and a symbol of Incan ingenuity. Read more about the Inca Empire.

3 Fun Facts

  • Machu Picchu was built without the use of mortar; stones fit together so precisely that even a knife cannot fit between them.
  • The site remained hidden from the outside world for over 400 years.
  • Machu Picchu is located about 2,430 meters above sea level.
